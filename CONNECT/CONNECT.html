MOOSE (Alces alces)
DID YOU KNOW
Moose primarily live in areas that have cold, snowy winters. Their wide hooves act like snowshoes to help them walk in the snow or in muddy, marshy ground after the white stuff melts.
A moose have the ability to kick in any direction with their front hooves? This is due to the flexibility of their shoulder joints, which allow them to move their hooves with ease and precision.

REINDEER(Rangifer tarandus)
DID YOU KNOW?
Reindeer are prominent in our holiday music and images. They pull Santa's sleigh, have red noses, have names, and fly.Measure for measure, reindeer are pretty awesome creatures. They are also threatened by global warming, oil exploration and other human-caused pressures.
In Europe, they are called reindeer. In North America, the animals are called caribou if they are wild and reindeer if they are domesticated. Both male and female reindeer grow Antlers where they are extensions of the skull grown by members of the reindeer family. Unlike horns, antlers are shed and regrown each year

PENGUINS(Spheniscidae)
DID YOU KNOW?
Penguins lay their eggs and care for their chicks on land. They usually mate for a long time and lay one or two eggs at a time. The parents take turns keeping their eggs warm and protecting and feeding their chicks once they hatch.
They are marine birds with paddle-like flippers used for swimming, not flying. Some spend up to 75% of their lives at sea. Penguins live south of the equator, from Antarctica to the Galapagos Islands.

NARWHAL (Monodon monoceros)
DID YOU KNOW?
The narwhal tusk is an enlarged tooth with sensory capability that can grow up to 10 feet in length. It is mostly found on male narwhals and has up to 10 million nerve endings inside. Some narwhals have up to two tusks, while others have none.
They are often dubbed the unicorns of the sea, narwhals are strange and beautiful creatures with long tusks.

MUSK OX
DID YOU KNOW?
Their name derives from their musky odour and from their superficial resemblance to the ox, though they are not closely related to cattle.
Musk oxen are animals that live in the cold Arctic and wander around in search of roots, mosses, and lichens to eat. They use their hooves to dig through snow and find these plants during winter. In summer, they eat Arctic flowers and grasses, and often feed near water. However, climate change has affected their habitat, enabling parasites and lungworms to expand their range in the musk oxen environment. Winter food sources are also compromised as freezing rain hardens the snowpack, making it more difficult for the musk oxen to dig through snow to graze on these plants.

SNOWY OWL
DID YOU KNOW?
Snowy owls have excellent eyesight, but they obviously can't see their prey when it's underneath snow or a thick layer of plants. To capture those meals, the owl relies on its other keen sense: hearing.
When you see a snowy owl, it's clear how the bird probably got its name: they're snow-white. Males are generally whiter than females. As males grow older, they get whiter. The females never become completely white—remaining brownish with darker markings.

GREENDLAND SHARK
DID YOU KNOW?
Greenland sharks are known to live at the ice edge and to live at the sea surface. They are also known to live in very deep waters down to at least 7200 feet (2200 m). Some people believe that they may enter freshwater and may even be a contributor to the Loch Ness Monster myth..
Greenland shark is one of the largest sharks in the ocean. Though both large and predatory, this species is not known to be particularly aggressive and is thought to be fairly sluggish in the cold waters of the north Atlantic Ocean.

BOWHEAD WHALE
DID YOU KNOW?
Their name derives from their extremely large heads and stocky bodies.Bowhead whales have a dark body with a distinctive white chin and unlike most cetaceans, do not have a dorsal fin.
Bowhead whales are one of the few whale species that reside almost exclusively in Arctic and subarctic waters experiencing seasonal sea ice coverage, primarily between 60° and 75° north latitude. Of all large whales, the bowhead is the most adapted to life in icy water. Adaptations to this environment include an insulating layer of blubber that can be up to 1.6 feet thick.

ARCTIC WOLF
DID YOU KNOW?
The Arctic wolf is able to withstand sub-zero temperatures and up to 5 months of absolute darkness a year. During the winter the Arctic wolf grows a second layer of fur for protection against the harsh conditions.
Thanks to its isolation, the arctic wolf also called the White Wolf of the north is not threatened by hunting and habitat destruction like its southern relatives. In fact, the arctic wolf is the only sub-species of wolf that is not threatened.
